About me:

A 22-year-old multidisciplinary artist and designer from Pune, Maharashtra — currently wrapping up my Graduation Project in Ceramic and Glass Design at NID Ahmedabad.

One of my biggest inspirations is nature, in all its glory and beauty. I am also deeply influenced by the rich history of creativity in India — Mughal architecture, ancient carvings, folk paintings, and textile prints.

I believe that indigenous techniques of working with materials are the core knowledge all designers need. My goal is to create designs that are inspired by the past, relevant to the present, and sustainable for the future.

NID · Product Design

Tangram for Motor & Sensory Development

An exploration into tactile play objects that support motor skill development and sensory engagement in early childhood. Designed using a material-first approach — walnut, copper, leather — to heighten material awareness and invite touch.

Tangram Photography
Project 2 · Research

Research & Inspiration

The project began with an extensive study of existing therapeutic play objects and Montessori learning tools. Research revealed that children aged 2–6 benefit most from objects that demand grip variation, weight awareness, and visual pattern recognition — simultaneously developing fine motor skills and cognitive sorting.

The traditional Chinese tangram — seven flat shapes that assemble into a square — proved the ideal structural starting point. Its constraint-based logic forces spatial reasoning while the material differentiation layer adds a sensory dimension absent in most commercial variants.

Project 2 · Design intent

Material-first approach

Rather than treating material as finish, each piece was assigned a distinct substance based on its intended sensory contribution. Walnut brings warmth and smoothness; hammered copper introduces a cool, tactile surface requiring careful handling; undyed leather provides grip contrast and a pliable, yielding resistance unfamiliar to most children's objects.

The interplay of these three materials across the seven geometric pieces creates an implicit hierarchy that guides play without explicit instruction — a core principle drawn from Montessori pedagogy.

Material
Explorations

Each material was sourced from regional Indian craftspeople — walnut from Kashmir, copper from Rajasthani workshops, leather from Dharavi's natural-tan tanneries. This grounding in local supply chains not only reduced the carbon footprint of the project but ensured each material carried authentic craft character rather than industrial uniformity.

Project 3 · Techniques

Surface Technique Studies

The course required systematic exploration of at least six distinct ceramic surface decoration methods. Each study was conducted as a controlled experiment — identical forms treated differently — allowing direct comparison of visual and tactile outcomes.

Engobe slip painting was ultimately selected for the final collection due to its compatibility with my design vocabulary: fluid, gestural, and rooted in the painting traditions of Indian folk crafts.

Technique 01 Engobe Slip Painting Coloured clay slips applied over leather-hard greenware, creating a matt painted surface post-glaze.
Technique 02 Sgraffito Incising through a layer of slip to reveal the contrasting clay body beneath — crisp and graphic.
Technique 03 Wax Resist Hot wax applied to define areas that resist the glaze, producing sharp negative-space patterns.
Technique 04 Terrasigillata Ultra-fine clay particle slip burnished onto the surface for a satin sheen without glaze.
4
NID · Print & Pattern

Print Design

An exploration of pattern and print as a surface language — drawing from Indian textile traditions and translating them into contemporary ceramic surface applications. The work bridges heritage craft techniques with modern material sensibilities.

Print Photography

Pattern
Development

The pattern work began with primary research — sketching Mughal floral motifs at the Sidi Saiyyed Mosque in Ahmedabad, recording block-print stamps at the Bagru workshops in Rajasthan, and photographing textile repeat structures across Delhi's Chandni Chowk. These were then translated into a scalable repeat system adaptable to flat ceramic surface application.

Project 4 · Process

From Heritage to Surface

Each pattern went through three translation stages: field documentation → digital abstraction → material application. The abstraction stage was critical — stripping the source motif to its essential geometry while retaining cultural legibility. A Mughal jaali lattice becomes a ceramic grid; a Bagru floral stamp becomes a slip-trail repeat.

The final collection of four prints was applied across a family of 150×150 mm stoneware tiles, demonstrating how the same pattern adapts to different glaze palettes and firing temperatures.

Project 4 · Outcomes

Final Application

The four patterns were produced as screen-print transfers and tested on both earthenware and stoneware bodies. Thermal expansion differences required pattern scaling adjustments between clay bodies — an unexpected technical constraint that informed a deeper understanding of print-to-surface material compatibility.

A secondary outcome was a set of design guidelines for adapting traditional Indian textile prints to ceramic surfaces — submitted as documentation to the NID library as a reference for future students.
